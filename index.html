Demographic Blindness Index v2 · 2000–2024

How well can a state see its own population? DBI combines census staleness and civil-registration incompleteness into a transparent, annual gauge of denominator reliability for 100 countries. When the count is wrong, every per-capita number — coverage rates, transfers, aid allocation — inherits the error.

Why it matters

Demographic blindness is not an accounting curiosity — it moves money. In Nigeria, stale and uneven population counts plausibly misdirect ~$105M / year in FAAC-style per-capita transfers toward the wrong states (ranged, illustrative). Across an allocation pool, the structural welfare loss from misallocation runs on the order of ~7.5–22.5% of the pool, scaling with how unevenly blindness is spread.

How blindness costs: it is the dispersion of blindness across units — not its average level — that misallocates. Uniform error cancels in per-capita shares; uneven error does not.

Methodology

DBI = 0.60·D2 + 0.40·D4, both in [0,1].
D2 census staleness = (year − last census)/40 · UN Statistics Division
D4 registration incompleteness = 1 − birth-registration rate · UNICEF/DHS
90% intervals from Monte-Carlo perturbation of weights and the normalisation anchor. Rises as a census ages; resets when a new census lands. Complement to (not a substitute for) the World Bank SPI: it isolates the denominator problem that capacity indices do not.
